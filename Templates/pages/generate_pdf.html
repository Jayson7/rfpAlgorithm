PREECLAMPSIA



Plan:
Consider the use of acetylsalicylic acid (aspirin) during pregnancy. Generally, a dose of 75-100 mg is recommended starting from the first trimester, preferably before the 16th week of gestation, and continue its use throughout the pregnancy. If the patient weighs more than 90 kg, increasing the dose to 150 mg per day may be considered. Follow-up: by your obstetrician and midwife. It is important to note that the final decision to initiate aspirin use, as well as determining the appropriate dosage, should be made by the obstetrician or specialist in charge of the patient. The doctor will evaluate the situation on an individual basis and consider the need for additional complementary tests before making a final decision.

{% endif %} {% if thrombosis %}

THROMBOSIS

Plan:
Consider the use of low molecular weight heparin at therapeutic doses from the beginning of pregnancy until at least 6 weeks after delivery. The dose and type of heparin will depend on the patient's weight, as well as other factors that your specialist will assess. Follow-up: It is recommended to be monitored by your obstetrician, midwife, and haematologist. General recommendations: Avoid dehydration by moderating caffeine and tea consumption and maintaining good hydration. It is also recommended to encourage mobility and engage in moderate exercise throughout pregnancy. Additionally, it is important to mention that if you are overweight or obese, working towards a healthy weight can help reduce the risk of thrombosis during pregnancy. Considerations before starting treatment: It is essential to consider other specific considerations for certain cases, such as patients with a history of previous bleeding, bleeding disorders or diseases, anticoagulant treatment, antiphospholipid syndrome, antithrombin deficiency, lymphoma, or sickle cell disease, among others. These cases require strict control and management of anticoagulation by the haematologist. It is important to remember that this recommendation should be evaluated and personalized by the specialist physician in charge of the patient, considering their individual clinical situation.

GESTATIONAL DIABETES

Plan:
If the patient is not yet pregnant, she would benefit from a simple glucose test and/or measurement of glycated hemoglobin A1c to assess glycemic function. If our patient is pregnant at the time of the questionnaire, the first-trimester blood test usually includes a measurement of fasting glucose. If the fasting glucose values are at the upper limit of normal, an oral glucose tolerance test in the first trimester should be considered to rule out gestational diabetes. Follow-up: It is important to note that the follow-up and management of these test results should be conducted by your obstetrician. In the case of a previous or gestational diabetes mellitus diagnosis, it is recommended to refer to a high-risk obstetrician and an endocrinology specialist for management during pregnancy. General recommendations: Maintaining an appropriate weight both before and during pregnancy is essential. If the patient is overweight or obese, working towards a healthy weight is recommended as it can help reduce the risk of developing diabetes. Additionally, it is important to maintain a balanced diet, rich in nutritious foods and low in added sugars. Regular physical exercise can also help prevent the onset of gestational diabetes.

THYROID DISORDER

Plan:
Consider performing thyroid function tests, such as measuring thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) and, if necessary, thyroxine (T4) levels, either preconception or at the beginning of pregnancy if you are already pregnant. Follow-up: It is important to have appropriate follow-up, and if the test results indicate values consistent with clinical hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, a high-risk obstetrician and an endocrinologist should be consulted to develop a therapeutic plan during pregnancy.

ANEMIA

Plan:
Consider performing a ferritin test in addition to haemoglobin measurement, either before pregnancy or during the first trimester if you are already pregnant. If ferritin levels are below 30mg/dL, consider starting oral iron supplementation. Follow-up: It is important to have regular follow-up with your obstetrician. In the case of pre-existing haematological conditions such as thalassemia, sickle cell disease, or hemoglobinopathies, or if there is a history of significant bleeding, a referral to haematology should be made.

HYPEREMESIS GRAVIDARUM

Plan:
It is important to be aware of situations in which you should seek specialist attention to prevent more serious complications arising from hyperemesis gravidarum. You should seek urgent medical attention if you experience persistent vomiting, cannot adequately hydrate yourself, have dark urine, have not urinated in the last 8 hours, vomit blood, or experience significant weight loss. Follow-up: with your obstetrician.

INTRAHEPATIC CHOLESTASIS

Plan:
It is essential to recognize the initial symptoms of intrahepatic cholestasis during pregnancy to facilitate early diagnosis and prevent significant complications. If you experience itching on hands and feet, especially at night and in the third trimester, it is important to communicate this to your obstetrician. However, these symptoms can appear from week 8 of pregnancy, so it is important to mention them to your obstetrician for a blood test that includes the measurement of bile acids. Additionally, you should be alert to other less common symptoms such as dark urine, pale stools, or jaundice, and if you experience any of them, you should consult your obstetrician immediately. Follow-up: with your obstetrician.
